### I. BASIS FOR AMENDED DEMAND

The original claim documented that H&R Block preparers (Weissgerber, PTIN P00694437; Worman) filed Form 1116 Foreign Tax Credit claims for tax years 2021–2024 that overstated foreign taxes paid by approximately 500%, claiming $30,000 against an actual Greek tax obligation (ENFIA) of $5,984. The 2024 Form 1116 contained a reported foreign source income of negative $830,000 — a figure that is computationally impossible for a retired widow with no foreign business activity, no foreign employment, and no foreign investments.

An independent preparer (Tracy Liao, Greenback Expat Tax Services) filed an amended return (1040-X) for TY2024 on April 10, 2026, zeroing the $30,000 FTC claim. The IRS accepted this amendment (Submission ID 3891692026100017w2tu). The original return is false on its face.

---

### II. RESPONDENT COUNSEL'S ISC DEFENSE OF THE -$830,000 FIGURE

At the ISC on April 15, 2026, respondent counsel affirmatively defended the negative $830,000 foreign income entry as a possible or plausible figure. This statement is noted for the record.

This defense constitutes an **affirmative litigation position** that a negative $830,000 in foreign source income is a defensible entry on a U.S. federal tax return for a retired widow residing in Roseville, Michigan, with zero foreign business operations, zero foreign employment income, and zero foreign investment activity.

Respondent counsel's defense of this figure carries the following consequences:

1. **IRC §7434 — Civil Damages for Fraudulent Filing of Information Returns.** The original -$830,000 entry establishes the predicate false information. Respondent counsel's ISC defense of the figure as plausible forecloses any future argument that the entry was a mere clerical error or software glitch. A figure that counsel defends in a settlement conference is a figure that the respondent stands behind.

2. **AAA Consumer Rule R-47 — Punitive Damages.** R-47 authorizes punitive damages where claimant demonstrates willful, wanton, or reckless conduct. The R-47 claim is now supported by three separate acts by three separate actors in connection with the same impossible figure:
   - **Act 1:** Worman filed the return containing -$830,000 (preparer conduct).
   - **Act 2:** Bree Cox (H&R Block Executive Escalations, Case 26236541) denied that any preparer assisted with the return, despite Weissgerber's PTIN appearing on the filing (corporate conduct).
   - **Act 3:** Christina Wahl defended the -$830,000 figure at the ISC as a plausible entry (litigation conduct).

   The progression from filing error to corporate denial to litigation defense of the same impossible figure establishes a pattern that a reasonable arbitrator may find constitutes willful or reckless disregard for the accuracy of a federal tax return prepared for a consumer.
