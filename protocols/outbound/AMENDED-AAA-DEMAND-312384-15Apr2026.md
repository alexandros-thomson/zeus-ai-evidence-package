## IV. THE THREE-ACTOR CHAIN: FROM INDIVIDUAL NEGLIGENCE TO INSTITUTIONAL WILLFULNESS

The original demand described what appeared to be an isolated filing error. Events since that filing — culminating in the ISC of April 15, 2026 — have disclosed a successive chain of institutional authentication that transforms the character of this claim.

Three separate H&R Block-affiliated representatives have now independently encountered and ratified the -$830,000 foreign-source income entry:

1. **Mellissa Worman (H&R Block Tax Preparer)** — *Filed it.* Worman prepared and filed the return containing the -$830,000 entry on Claimant's Form 1116. Whether through error or design, the entry was submitted to the IRS under Worman's PTIN.

2. **Bree Cox (H&R Block Executive Escalations)** — *Approved it.* Upon escalation by Claimant through H&R Block's internal resolution channels, Cox reviewed the entry and declined to correct it, thereby institutionally ratifying Worman's submission.

3. **Christina M. Wahl (Counsel, BerkowitzOliver LLP, representing Respondent HRB Digital LLC)** — *Defended it.* At the ISC on April 15, 2026, Wahl, as Respondent's counsel and authorized representative, characterized the -$830,000 figure as "possible" before the AAA in the context of settlement discussions. This is not merely a litigation posture; it is a third-party, counsel-authenticated endorsement of the underlying entry in an adversarial proceeding.

This chain — preparer filed, escalations approved, outside counsel defended — eliminates any plausible claim that the -$830,000 entry was an isolated clerical mistake. It is the product of a consistent institutional response across three independent actors. That is the definition of willful conduct under IRC §7434.

## V. THE -$830,000 ENTRY: A MATHEMATICAL IMPOSSIBILITY

For the avoidance of doubt, and for the record before the AAA:

Negative foreign-source income is not a permissible outcome under 26 U.S.C. §901 or any provision of the Form 1116 instructions. The foreign tax credit mechanism operates on *positive* foreign-source income. A figure of -$830,000 on Form 1116, line 1(a) or the equivalent, has no valid computational origin. It does not represent a carryback. It does not represent a loss carryforward in the Form 1116 context. It is not a recognized result of any legitimate tax calculation involving Claimant's financial circumstances.

The IRS confirmed this when it accepted Claimant's Form 1040-X, which corrected the foreign-source income figure to $0.

No valid scenario — no preparer methodology, no software output, no interpretive framework — produces -$830,000 as a legitimate result for a 79-year-old widow with a modest Greek real-property interest and a fixed U.S. income. Respondent's counsel's characterization of this figure as "possible" is, with respect, not defensible.
